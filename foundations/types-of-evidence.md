# Types of Evidence 

Evidence is any material, information, or observation that can be used 
to help establish facts in a legal investigation. In forensic science, 
evidence is analyzed to determine its significance and how it may relate 
to people, places, or events. 

Understanding how evidence is classified is essential, because different 
types of evidence are collected, analyzed, and interpreted in different ways. 

--- 

## Physical Evidence 
Physical evidence includes any **tangible object or material** that can 
be collected from a scene and examined. 

Examples of physical evidence include: 
- Fingerprints 
- Blood and other biological materials 
- Hair and fibers 
- Weapons or tools 
- Glass, soil, or paint fragments 

Physical evidence is often considered highly valuable because it can be 
examined using scientific methods and does not rely on human memory. 

--- 

## Testimonial Evidence 
Testimonial evidence consists of **statements made by witnesses** under 
oath, such as in court or during sworn depositions. 
Examples include: 
- Eyewitness accounts 
- Victim statements 
- Expert testimony 
While testimonial evidence can provide important context, it is subject 
to limitations such as memory errors, bias, and perception differences. 
--- 

## Documentary Evidence 
Documentary evidence includes **written or recorded materials** that 
provide information about events or actions. 
Examples include: 
- Written documents 
- Photographs and videos 
- Audio recordings 
- Digital records, such as text messages or emails  
Documentary evidence may be physical or digital and often requires 
authentication to verify its origin and integrity. 

--- 
## Direct vs. Circumstantial Evidence 
Evidence can also be classified based on how it relates to the facts of a case. 

### Direct Evidence 
Direct evidence directly establishes a fact without requiring inference. 
Example: 
- A video recording showing an event as it occurred 

### Circumstantial Evidence 
Circumstantial evidence requires interpretation or inference to connect 
it to a conclusion. 
Example: 
- Fingerprints at a scene suggesting a person was present 
 
Both types of evidence are valid and commonly used in investigations. 

--- 
## Class Evidence vs. Individual Evidence 
Another critical distinction is between **class** and **individual** evidence. 

### Class Evidence 
Class evidence can be associated with a **group or category**, but not a 
single unique source. 
Examples: 
- Blood type 
- Shoe size 
- Fiber type 

### Individual Evidence 
Individual evidence can be linked to a **single, unique source** with a 
high degree of certainty. 
Examples: 
- DNA profiles 
- Fingerprints with sufficient unique characteristics 
This distinction affects how strongly evidence can be associated with a person 
or object. 

--- 
 
## Why Evidence Classification Matters 
Classifying evidence correctly helps forensic scientists: 
- Choose appropriate analysis methods 
- Interpret results accurately 
- Communicate findings clearly 
- Avoid overstating conclusions 
Misunderstanding evidence types can lead to incorrect assumptions or 
misinterpretation of forensic results. 
--- 
## Summary 
Evidence can be classified in multiple ways, including: 
- Physical, testimonial, and documentary 
- Direct and circumstantial 
- Class and individual 
Each classification provides important context for how evidence is analyzed 
and how conclusions should be interpreted.
