# Eyewitness Reliability & Memory 
Eyewitness testimony is often considered important in criminal 
investigations. However, research shows that **human memory and perception 
can be unreliable**, especially under stressful or unusual circumstances. 
Understanding these limitations is essential for interpreting testimonial 
evidence alongside physical evidence. 

--- 
## How Memory Works 
Memory is not like a video recording. Instead, it is: 
- **Constructive:** Our brains fill in gaps and make inferences   
- **Selective:** Attention focuses on some details while ignoring others   
- **Fallible:** Stress, fear, and distractions can distort what we remember 
Because of these factors, witnesses may **honestly report something 
that is inaccurate**. 

---

--- 
## Direct vs. Circumstantial Testimonial Evidence 
- **Direct testimony:** A witness directly observed an event   
- **Circumstantial testimony:** A witness infers what occurred based on observations   
Even accurate witnesses can provide **circumstantial evidence**, which requires careful interpretation alongside physical evidence. 
--- 

## Why This Matters in Forensics 
- Eyewitness accounts can be helpful but **cannot be taken as absolute proof**   
- Combining testimonial evidence with physical or biological evidence strengthens investigations   
- Forensic scientists and investigators must be aware of memory limitations when evaluating witness statements 
--- 
## Best Practices (High-Level) 
- Collect eyewitness statements **as soon as possible**   
- Ask open-ended questions, avoiding leading language   
- Verify consistency across multiple witnesses   
- Always corroborate with physical evidence when possible 
--- 
## Summary 
Eyewitness testimony is a valuable tool, but human memory is imperfect.   
Understanding its limitations helps forensic investigators interpret statements 
carefully and avoid overreliance on possibly flawed recollections.
